# Private Ocean Enforcement Fleet — Summary
### Research Report Condensed Summary

---

## The Scenario
A private group operating 3 medium-large ships (~50–80m each) that are fully ocean self-sufficient AND legally authorized to interdict illegal vessels. This is achievable — Sea Shepherd has done versions of this for decades.

---

## The Core Legal Answer

**You CANNOT legally board foreign-flagged ships on the high seas as a private party** — with two exceptions:
1. **Piracy** (UNCLOS Art. 105) — any vessel may seize a pirate ship on the high seas (universal jurisdiction)
2. **Embedded government authority** — if a national coast guard officer sails aboard your ship, that officer can board legally

**The Sea Shepherd Government Partnership Model** is the most viable approach:
- Embed officers from Gabon, Mexico, Pacific Island nations, etc. aboard your ships
- Now YOU have legal boarding authority in those nations' waters
- They provide enforcement credentials; you provide the ships, crew, and resources

## Where to Operate (Best Legal Opportunity)

**Pacific Island EEZ Enforcement** is the best opportunity:
- Nations like Kiribati, Palau, and Marshall Islands have massive EEZs and virtually no enforcement capacity
- They actively seek NGO partnerships
- You operate *within their EEZ* with *their officers aboard* = full legal authority
- IUU (illegal, unreported, unregulated) fishing costs $23 billion/year globally; political will to fight it is strong

## The 4 Laws You Need to Know

1. **UNCLOS Article 110** — Only warships can exercise "right of visit" on the high seas. Not private ships. *Unless...*
2. **UNCLOS Article 105** — Any nation can seize a pirate ship; piracy has universal jurisdiction.
3. **UNCLOS Article 101** — Piracy definition: violence for private ends, on high seas, against another ship. Drug trafficking ≠ piracy. IUU fishing ≠ piracy.
4. **Flag State Law** — Register your ships in sympathetic states. Netherlands, Palau, and several African nations have been cooperative with conservation enforcement NGOs.

---

## The 5 Steps to Make It Real

1. **Incorporate** as NGO in Netherlands or Palau; hire maritime law specialist
2. **Buy first vessel** (used OSV/research ship); hire experienced captain + crew
3. **Get first government partnership** (Palau, Kiribati, or similar Pacific island nation)
4. **Deploy with documentary crew** — media creates donations and legal accountability
5. **Expand fleet** as funding allows; second ship = fast pursuit; third = logistics

---

## The 3 Biggest Risks

1. **Legal exposure**: Without proper government partnership and legal review, your operations could be classified as piracy under international or US law
2. **Cost overruns**: Old vessel refits often cost far more than purchase price; budget 2x your refit estimate
3. **Diplomatic pressure**: Nations (China, Japan, others) have pressured small states to revoke NGO ship registrations — always have backup flag state relationships
